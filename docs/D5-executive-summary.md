# Executive Summary: Why Startups Fail, and How Long They Last

## The question

Looking at nearly 400 real startups that failed, can we predict how long a company will last before it fails — and can we tell, in advance, whether it's headed for an early collapse or a long, slow decline?

## What we found

**Timing is predictable, to a point.** Using basic facts about a company — how much money it raised, what industry it's in, when it was founded, and which of nine common failure reasons apply (competition, ran out of budget, poor fit with the market, and so on) — we can predict roughly how long it will survive, meaningfully better than just guessing the average.

**The simpler model won.** We tried a spectrum of models, from a naive guess up to a sophisticated one. The simplest genuinely useful model — one that makes decisions the way a person would, with a small number of clear rules — matched or beat the fancy one on real, held-out data. The complex model looked better on the data it was trained on, but that was memorization, not real skill.

**Two things matter far more than the rest.** How much money the company raised, and — more surprisingly — *when it was founded*, mattered more than any specific reason cited for failure. The era a startup was born into shaped its fate more than whether it faced a budget crunch, tough competition, or a market that didn't want the product.

**We built a live tool.** A web app lets you enter a hypothetical startup's details and see the model's prediction in real time, plus a dashboard showing how the models compare.

## What we recommend

- **Use the simple model, not the complex one.** It's just as accurate here and far easier for a non-technical audience to trust and explain.
- **Treat this as a pattern-finder, not a fortune teller.** The model explains what already-failed startups had in common — it should not be used to forecast the fate of a company that's still operating, since the "failure reasons" it learns from are only knowable in hindsight.
- **Don't treat any single reason as "the" cause.** The specific reasons cited (competition, poor market fit, etc.) explain less than funding and timing do — resist the urge to pick one villain.
